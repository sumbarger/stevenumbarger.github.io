Research

My current research focuses on understanding the evolution of galaxies through galaxy mergers. I utilize JWST observations to complete my analysis. However, in the past I have worked with star clusters in both high-resolution observations and cosmological simulations, Weak Carbon-Nitrogen stars, and made astronomy more accessible through the use of 3D models. Below is a summary of my current and past projects.

Keck Adaptive Optics vs. Hubble Imaging of Star Clusters in M31

I study the structure of star clusters in the Andromeda Galaxy (M31) by comparing high-resolution adaptive optics observations from the Keck Observatory with imaging from the Hubble Space Telescope. This project investigates how differences in instrumentation, resolution, and observing techniques affect measurements of cluster morphology.

Using Keck NIRC2 images and archival HST data, I perform image processing tasks such as sky subtraction and artifact removal, followed by detailed visual and quantitative comparisons. I use DS9 and 3D visualization tools to examine how cluster features appear across datasets and to assess the strengths and limitations of each telescope.

This work helps inform best practices for combining space-based and ground-based observations when studying dense stellar systems and provides insight into how observational choices influence our interpretation of star cluster structure.

Visualization of FIRE-2 Star Clusters

A central component of my research is the use of 3D visualization to better understand and communicate the structure of star clusters and galaxies. I create three-dimensional models from both observational data and simulations to explore spatial relationships that are difficult to capture in traditional two-dimensional images.

I use tools such as Astro3D and custom visualization scripts to transform astronomical datasets into interactive and physical models. These visualizations support scientific analysis while also serving as powerful tools for outreach and education.

By integrating visualization directly into the research process, I aim to bridge the gap between data analysis, interpretation, and public engagement.

Accessibility in Astronomy for the Visually Impaired

I am actively involved in an outreach-focused research project aimed at making astronomy more accessible to individuals with visual impairments. This work combines astronomical data with tactile 3D models and sound-based representations to create inclusive learning tools.

My contributions include generating 3D-printable models of star clusters and galactic structures, as well as collaborating on lesson plans that integrate these materials into educational settings. I am particularly interested in extending these methods to simulation data, allowing learners to physically explore theoretical models of galaxy evolution.

This project reflects my broader commitment to inclusive science and to ensuring that astronomical research and education are accessible to a diverse range of audiences.
